# Shape Up — Betting Table

The betting table is the decision point before each cycle. A small group (typically senior leaders
or decision-makers) reviews the available pitches and decides which ones to bet cycle time on.

## What Gets Bet

Only pitches go to the betting table — not ideas, tasks, or backlogs. A pitch must satisfy the
five-element structure before it is eligible.

## Betting Table Process

1. **Review available pitches** — read each pitch before the meeting, not during.
2. **Evaluate appetite fit** — does the team have the capacity to build this in the stated appetite?
3. **Assess risk** — are the rabbit holes addressed? Is the scope bounded?
4. **Decide** — bet or don't bet. No partial bets, no "let's see how it goes."
5. **Assign a team** — name the designer and programmer(s) for each bet.
6. **Set start date** — cycles start on a fixed date, not "when ready."

## What Happens to Unbetted Pitches

Unbetted pitches are not queued. They expire. If the problem is still worth solving, the pitch
can be revised and re-submitted for a future betting table. This is intentional: it keeps the
table from accumulating stale work.

## Appetite Setting Rules

| Appetite | When to use |
|---|---|
| Small batch (1-2 weeks) | Well-understood problems, small teams, low-risk scope |
| Big batch (6 weeks) | Complex problems requiring sustained focus; one team per bet |

Do not split a 6-week appetite into two 3-week chunks. Either it fits in 6 weeks or it needs
to be shaped differently.

## Betting Table Anti-Patterns

- **Carrying work over from the previous cycle** — re-pitch and re-bet instead.
- **Betting on an unfinished pitch** — "we'll figure it out during the cycle" is not a bet.
- **Multiple bets for one team** — each team gets one bet per cycle.
- **Betting on a backlog item** — backlogs are not pitches; shape the problem first.

## Team Sizing

Shape Up teams are small: typically one designer and one or two programmers per bet. Larger
teams signal that the problem is not well enough shaped or scoped.
